Abstract

AI products increasingly claim to know, remember, personalize to, and act on behalf of particular users, yet they are evaluated on transcription, memory retrieval, summarization, personalization, and conversational quality — none of which establishes whether longitudinal observation of a person yields calibrated predictive knowledge about that specific person. We call the missing capability personal intelligence, strictly as a bounded operational label: the capacity to forecast a specific person's future observable state transitions beyond population priors and beyond that person's own routine. TargetSpace is a benchmark protocol for that missing measurement: a system receives observations of a consenting target up to a sealed time t and issues probabilistic forecasts of the target's future observable state transitions; forecasts are timestamped and hashed before outcomes exist, outcomes resolve by deterministic rules, and scoring is strictly proper. Skill counts as target-specific only if it beats a population prior (R1), beats the target's own routine (R2), stays calibrated, and collapses when forecasts are scored against the wrong target. An evidence-tier ablation attributes any lift to the observations that produced it, treating observation quality as a first-class experimental variable rather than an unmeasured background condition. The protocol thereby operationalizes a broader question: which observations — modalities, sampling schedules, coverage — are sufficient for reliable target-specific prediction, and at what cost in energy, burden, and privacy. Evidence efficiency (gated skill per disclosed observation cost) and minimum sufficient observation organize that agenda. This is a pre-pilot protocol with a synthetic reference harness; no human-subject results are reported. A high score certifies calibrated predictive skill about future observable states, not understanding in any richer sense, inner life, causation, or permission to act.

Positioning and using TargetSpace

A summary of the paper's positioning section.

A complementary, under-measured axis

TargetSpace is not the "best world-model benchmark overall." It addresses a distinct, under-measured axis — target-specific forecasting under partial observation — that physical-plausibility, generation-fidelity, and control-success benchmarks do not, by construction, score.

Compared on shared axes only

Physical reasoning, video generation, embodied robotics, symbolic forecasting, and agent memory are complementary families. TargetSpace is compared with each only where they overlap — see the comparison table.

A battery of controls

Apply it as a protocol: zero/short/longitudinal history, shuffled-history and wrong-target controls, modality ablation, oracle and human anchors, calibration, and evidence attribution. See Baselines.

Positioning statement

TargetSpace evaluates a missing layer — whether a model can transform passive longitudinal observation into a target-specific predictive model — central for agents that reason about particular people, teams, systems, or environments over time.
